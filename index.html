01
Chronology
From Brick Lane youth collective to pioneers of the Asian Underground
1965 / 1968
The Shamshers Are Born
Haroon Shamsher born 14 November 1965; Farook Shamsher born 24 October 1968. Both born in Bradford, West Yorkshire to a Bangladeshi father (Soni Shamsher, a professional flautist) and an Indian mother. The family settles in the East End of London. Their father runs a Brick Lane shop selling saris and traditional instruments imported from India, and organises recording sessions with Baul artists. The brothers grow up watching Indian musicians record in a makeshift studio in the store and sell cassettes on the street.
Origins
1983
Joi Bangla Founded
Haroon and Farook conceive the "League of Joi Bangla Youth Organisation" — later shortened to Joi Bangla — a collective set up to promote Bengali culture to local children in the East End. They begin spinning records in youth clubs around the Brick Lane area, mixing traditional Bengali music with James Brown riffs, funky breakbeats, hip hop, and dancehall. The name "Joi Bangla" is a rallying cry from the 1971 Bangladesh liberation movement. They quickly establish themselves as the best DJs and party organisers in East London.
Founding Community
1986
First Music Video
Joi Bangla's first music video is produced at Despite TV — an East London video collective operating out of the Tower Hamlets Arts Project on Whitechapel Road — while the group is still without a recording contract. Originally intended for the Local Band slot of the Despite TV magazine, it was never released at the time. The full 20-minute version exists in the Spectacle distribution archive.
Film Archive
1988
First Recordings
Joi releases their first white label single "Taj Ma House" on BPM Records, followed by "Funky Asian". They begin introducing their own material into Joi Bangla sets. Arts Council funding helps bolster the project, which evolves into the more dance-oriented "Joi" name. They are playing bass clef and other East London venues including the venue that will later become the Blue Note in Hoxton Square — then in an area so rough that, as Farook recalls, it was "hide-your-money-in-your-socks" territory.
First Releases
1990
I'm British But... — First Film Appearance
Director Gurinder Chadha (later of Bend It Like Beckham) casts Haroon as himself — credited as "Self – Joi Bangla Sound" — in her documentary I'm British But..., a portrait of young British Asians navigating dual identity. The first significant on-camera document of Joi in performance, now held in the BFI archive.
Film
1989
Rebranded as Joi
After six years as Joi Bangla Sound System, the collective shifts to the more dance-oriented name "Joi". The change reflects their expanding musical scope while maintaining the community ethos. By now they are active members of a growing Asian dance scene and have moved from daytime community gigs to club nights promoting Asian music in general.
Evolution
1992
"Desert Storm" — Breakout Single
Joi releases "Desert Storm" on Transglobal. The NME names it Single of the Week, calling it "one of the most inventive dance records ever made". It travels internationally and becomes their signature early track. Later included as the opening bonus track on the 2015 best-of compilation. (Note: A Desert Storm appears on a 2024 Full Circle compilation "Kicking Dust: The Goa Way", confirming its enduring reach into psychedelic trance circles.)
Milestone NME Single of the Week
1993
Joi Club Night Launches
Joi sets up their own club night in London. Their Thursday night "Asian Vibes" at the Bass Clef (later Blue Note) in Hoxton Square becomes a nerve centre of what will become the Asian Underground movement. Regular crowd includes hippie types, Bangladeshi teenagers, and a mix of cultures. Björk is a regular guest; a young Gerri Halliwell does a brief stint as a Joi dancer. Nitin Sawhney, Talvin Singh and the future founders of Asian Dub Foundation are all in orbit.
Club Culture Asian Underground
1994
Management & Growth
Joi appoints Charles Cosh as their manager (later co-managed with Ben Batson of Moksha Management Ltd; New York bookings via Sam Kirby of Evolution Talent Agency). By this point they are doing bi-monthly nights at the Dog Star in Brixton, queues around the block.
Industry
1996
Bangladesh EP + WOMAD + Real World
Joi releases the Bangladesh EP on Nation Records (Aki Nawaz's label). They perform at WOMAD, where they catch the attention of Peter Gabriel. Gabriel invites them to sign to Real World Records — "Welcome to the family," he says. They are among the very few British Asian artists to make it onto the Real World roster. They also remix "Sweet Pain" by Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan for the Star Rise tribute album in 1997.
Real World WOMAD
1997
Signed to Real World
Officially sign to Real World Records — the label Farook describes as "the only label we felt happy to be involved with long term". Major label interest had come and gone with what Farook describes as a "brief, ill-fated signing". Real World represented something different: a home for global music with artistic integrity. Joi had by now done over 1,500 gigs as a sound system. Their live band features vocalist Susheela Raman, guitarist Vik Sharma, and percussionist Bongo Paul, plus traditional instruments — tabla, sitar, flute — over driving techno rhythms.
Real World
1998
Spiritualized Tour + "Fingers" Single
Joi supports Spiritualized on their British tour. Jason Pierce (Jason Spaceman) later recalls the brothers' "welcoming and non-judgemental friendship" when he first moved to London. Limited edition single "Fingers" released December 1998, with a Justin Robertson (Lionrock) remix that becomes popular on the club scene and paves the way for the debut album.
Live
1999 (March)
One and One Is One — Debut Album
Released April 1999 on Real World. Named after a Rabindranath Tagore metaphysical equation on love and friendship. Hailed across the board: Q magazine gives it four stars; NME calls it "inventive, hard-hitting." The album blends melodic harmonies, tabla rhythms, electro beats, chants, and vocal snatches. Their music is used in Sex and the City. They headline The Viper Room in Los Angeles at Johnny Depp's invitation. Wins BBC Asian Music Award.
BBC Asian Music Award Debut Album
1999 (July 8)
Haroon Shamsher Dies
Haroon dies unexpectedly at age 34 of a heart attack. He had been ill for a week and suffered the fatal attack during a hospital examination — having performed the night before. Two months earlier he had visited Bangladesh for a month, making field recordings of local musicians in the family's village in Sylhet, including tabla players, flute players, and a 14-year-old girl from the village who sang directly to him. Those recordings become the foundation of the next album. Farook later says: "I still feel he is there, that he left behind something that is part of both of us."
Passing
2000
Eurythmics European Stadium Tour
Annie Lennox personally invites Joi to support Eurythmics on a European stadium tour, playing to 15–20,000 nightly. The tour runs while Farook is completing We Are Three in the immediate aftermath of Haroon's death. It is one of the largest audiences Joi has ever played to, and the first major international engagement for the post-Haroon configuration.
Live Eurythmics
2000–2001
We Are Three — Tribute Album
Released October 2000 (some sources say 2001), built entirely around Haroon's Bangladesh field recordings and their unreleased collaborations. Co-produced by Farook Shamsher, John Coxon and Ashley Wales (Spring Heel Jack). The title references a passage from the Sufi poet Rumi. Album opens with the sounds of a rickshaw journey from Sylhet town to their village. Farook tours Europe with the Eurythmics, playing to 15–20,000 nightly. Reviews in Q (4 stars), Wired USA ("one of the most dynamic worldbeat releases of the year"), London Metro.
Second Album Bangladesh
2003
Featured in "Mutiny: Asians Storm British Music"
Vivek Bald's documentary on the Asian Underground movement, premiering at San Francisco Asian-American Film Festival (reviewed in Variety, March 2003). Survey of the impact Asian-heritage music had on British pop. Joi are central figures in the story, alongside Asian Dub Foundation, FunDaMental, Cornershop, Talvin Singh.
Documentary Legacy
2007
Without Zero — Third Album
Released January 2007. Named after Hindu mathematician Brahmagupta (born 598 BC), credited with the concept of zero. Farook describes it as a fusion of both previous albums, with a new Arabic influence. Introduces Mumbai-based sitarist Niladri Kumar ("Asian Hendrix") after Farook ditched most of his written material upon hearing him play. Features Algerian multi-instrumentalist Yazid on zorna, oud and banjo; vocalist Apeksha Dandekar. Wins Farook the UK Asian Music Awards Commitment to Scene award in 2006.
Third Album UK Asian Music Award
2015
Joi Sound System — Best Of
Real World releases a 22-track double album compiled from all three studio albums plus three bonus tracks, including "Desert Storm". The liner notes by Jane Cornwell contextualise Joi's full arc: "Joi weren't simply a band or a sound system. Joi were a way of thinking. A philosophy."
Compilation
2017
Haroon Honoured with Blue Plaque
On 15 June 2017, the British Plaque Trust (in partnership with BBC Asian Network / BBC Music Day) unveils a Blue Plaque for Haroon Shamsher in Brick Lane — one of the first Blue Plaques awarded to a British South Asian artist. The announcement prompts an outpouring from figures including Nitin Sawhney, Jason Spaceman (Spiritualised), Pandit G (Asian Dub Foundation), Jon Coxon (Spring Heel Jack), Aki Nawaz (Nation Records), Colin Angus (The Shamen), and Bobby Friction.
Blue Plaque Legacy
2026
Farook on Worldwide FM
Farook joins Rohan Rakhit's First Light show on Worldwide FM (Gilles Peterson's station) on 21 April 2026 — described as "producer, DJ and activist... one of the key figureheads of the Asian Underground scene of the 90s and 2000s". Confirms Farook is still active and engaged with contemporary music culture.

Key People
The Shamsher brothers and their extended circle
Haroon Shamsher
Co-Founder · DJ · Visionary
The elder brother and original DJ engine of Joi. Farook describes him as "the Godfather of DJing and professional mixing." Technically extraordinary — capable of synchronising two digital tapes by hand, achieving polyrhythms that mixed Asian music with Western beats with total precision. Characterised by collaborators as shy, quiet, and resistant to the limelight — "very loud qualities that most others in that microcosm seemed to lack" (Mushtaq Omar Uddin). Visited Bangladesh two months before his death to capture field recordings for what became We Are Three. Died July 8, 1999 of heart failure, age 34.
14 Nov 1965 — 8 Jul 1999 · Brick Lane Blue Plaque, 2017
DJField RecordingFounder
Farook Shamsher
Co-Founder · Producer · Activist · Continuing Member
The younger brother and sole continuing member of Joi. Farook describes himself as "the spice chef — I grind the spices. The master chefs put it all together. But I have the final say on how it tastes." Has continued Joi's mission as a DJ, producer, and cultural activist. Still refers to Joi in the collective and occasionally speaks of Haroon in the present tense. Called "Godfather of London's Asian Underground" by Rolling Stone India. Became a father circa 2009. Most recently appeared on Worldwide FM's First Light with Rohan Rakhit (April 2026).
Born 24 Oct 1968, Bradford

Soni Shamsher
Father · Professional Flautist
The brothers' father — a professional flautist of Bangladeshi origin who ran a Brick Lane shop selling saris and traditional instruments imported from the Indian subcontinent. Organised sessions and recorded with Baul artists, selling the tapes. His impromptu studio became the environment where Farook and Haroon first absorbed the sonic vocabulary that would define Joi. Farook credits his father alongside Haroon as the foundational influence on his life in music.

Niladri Kumar
Sitarist — Without Zero
Mumbai-based sitarist dubbed "Asian Hendrix" by collaborators. A student of Ravi Shankar, he reinvents sitar rhythms by fusing classical mastery with modern sensibility. When Farook heard him play, he discarded most of his pre-written material for Without Zero and started again. Kumar told Farook: "I've played for you like I've never played for anyone else." His playing dominates the album's sonic character.

05
Context & Scene
The world Joi built, and the world that built Joi

Brick Lane & The East End

Joi emerges from the specific geography of Brick Lane — the commercial and cultural heart of East London's Bangladeshi community since the 1970s. Their father's shop is a node of musical exchange; the street itself is a battleground. The early 1980s East End is marked by racism, NF activity, and poverty. "We used to go through certain areas and get abuse thrown at us," Farook recalls. "That was basically why Joi was set up — to combat that sort of stuff and to say that we're just the same as you."

The choice to set up a club in Hoxton Square — "real hide-your-money-in-your-socks stuff" — before it became fashionable was both a political act and a pragmatic one: no mainstream venues would have them. Joi didn't move to where culture was; they created culture where they were.

The Asian Underground

Joi predates the term "Asian Underground" by nearly a decade. They are its origin point, not its product. Talvin Singh later sets up Anokha — the weekly club night at the Blue Note credited with coining the scene — in the same venue where Joi had already been running their Thursday nights. Nitin Sawhney, Asian Dub Foundation, Cornershop, FunDaMental, and Cornershop all orbit the same East London network.

The scene emerges from a generation born-and-raised in Britain who reject both their parents' traditionalism and the racism of mainstream British society — embracing punk's DIY ethic while finding their sonic language in hip hop, drum 'n' bass, dub, bhangra, ragas, and qawwali simultaneously. Joi's particular contribution is the insistence on bringing their parents into it: getting Arts Council funding, involving classical musicians, maintaining the community ethos even as the music became harder and more electronic.

Nitin Sawhney: "Haroon was a pioneer of sound, a pioneer of social conscience and awareness and a pioneer of Asian street credibility in Britain. He and his brother Farook inspired myself and many others, including Talvin Singh, to search further and harder for new sounds to express a dispossessed generation of British Asians. If there ever was an Asian underground sound, it started with Joi."

The Philosophy

Joi's three album titles encode a deliberate intellectual project: Tagore (love and unity), Rumi (spiritual community), Brahmagupta (Asian genius unrecognised in the West). Farook: "There are so many Asian geniuses that are still unsung in the West. We want to engage peoples' imaginations, get them thinking and exploring." Their T-shirts read "Set yourself free with the spirit of Joi." Their message is anti-racist but not grievance-based — it is centred on abundance, beauty, and the revelation of shared human heritage.

The mix methodology reflects this: Farook describes DJing as gradually introducing a traditional Bengali tune into a James Brown groove — slowly raising the volume of the traditional sound until the crowd are dancing to it alone, often without realising the shift has happened. This is not assimilation; it is seduction toward roots.

Sound System vs. Live Band

Joi operated simultaneously as a sound system and as a full live band — an unusual dual identity. As a sound system they mixed vinyl, DATs, samples and live tabla improvisation. As a band they added Susheela Raman on vocals, Vik Sharma on guitar, Bongo Paul on percussion, plus flute, sitar. "Which people still aren't doing," Farook notes of the practice of mixing DATs with vinyl while playing live over the top. They headlined WOMAD, Tribal Gathering, Whirl-Y-Gig, Big Chill, Glastonbury, Wembley, and performed across Europe and the US. By 1998, over 1,500 gigs as a sound system.

The Geri Halliwell Story

At a legendary Joi appearance at the Astoria in London, a then-unknown Geri Halliwell turned up uninvited and offered to dance. Farook recalls: "Because of our Eastern sound, she turned up in this skimpy leotard and did this belly-dancing routine. We were like: er, we're not really that kind of band. Will you go away and put some clothes on please? And she did, thank God." She would go on to become Ginger Spice. The anecdote captures both the cultural magnetism of early Joi and the limits of how their music was being read from the outside.

The Andy Weatherall & The Orb Connection

In the early 1990s, Joi's Deep Asian Vibes sessions at the Bass Clef featured fellow DJs Andy Weatherall (Sabres of Paradise) and Alex Paterson of The Orb. This cross-pollination — between the white rave underground and the Asian sound system world — is often overlooked in accounts of the period. Joi were not operating in a sealed ethnic scene; they were at the centre of a wider experimental underground that crossed race and genre lines from the beginning.

Joi Bangla as Incubator

The Joi Bangla Sound System functioned as a training ground for figures who went on to shape the wider scene. Sam Zaman — later known as State of Bengal, "Godfather of Asian Breakbeat" — started out with the Shamsher brothers before going solo. He also mentored his younger brother Deeder, who became a rapper for Asian Dub Foundation. The network radiating out from Joi Bangla includes Nation Records (Aki Nawaz), Anokha (Talvin Singh, Sweety Kapoor), and much of the creative infrastructure of 1990s British Asian music.

9/11 and the Scene's End

All key figures in the Asian Underground identify September 2001 as the rupture point that ended the movement's mainstream momentum. As Nitin Sawhney tells Mixmag: "9/11 shifted a whole psychology, there was a lot of fear after that and a lot of political opportunism. Racists look for any excuse to justify being racist." Many leftfield Asian artists were dropped from major labels in the following years; media interest evaporated. DJ Ritu: "After 9/11, a lot of the latent racism that had always been there rose to the surface again." The Blue Plaque campaign for Haroon and the State of Bengal (led by Sweety Kapoor and supporters petitioning the British Plaque Trust) is partly a corrective to this erasure — an attempt to write the Asian Underground back into British cultural history on its own terms.

The Washington Post (January 2001)

Alona Wartofsky's review of We Are Three in the Washington Post describes Joi as "a gifted DJ duo blending contemporary dance-music electronics with traditional Bengali styles" — the first significant American press coverage of the group, published six months after Haroon's death. The review situates the album in the context of the brothers' upbringing above their father's Bengali-music store in East London, where they were simultaneously immersed in reggae, hip-hop, soul, and Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan. The piece marks a rare moment of mainstream American attention to the Asian Underground.

Two months before his death, Haroon travelled to Sylhet, Bangladesh — to the family's village — with a mini-disc recorder. He spent a month capturing: flute players, tabla players, a 14-year-old girl from the village who sang directly to him. He recorded the sounds of a rickshaw journey from Sylhet town to the village. He recorded a Bengali man intoning that one should never forget. These recordings became We Are Three. Farook returned to Bangladesh in June 2000 to continue the work: "It's brilliant to see so many people in Sylhet who've heard of Joi's music." The Bangladesh thread runs through Joi's entire work — the village, the father, the musicians, the language.

So suddenly to be in a situation where white people as well as Asians and Black people were coming to these club nights — they're jumping up and down to sitars and Indian Classical singers. Those nights felt full of optimism. It felt like anything was possible.
Nitin Sawhney — Mixmag 2021
It wasn't just the music, it was across the creative arts, it was political. It was a coming of age, a paradigm shift. This was a group of South Asian kids saying, "You don't get to define us. You don't get to write our narratives, because we're telling them ourselves."
Sweety Kapoor — Mixmag 2021
We wanted to give our own people a sense of identity. It was seen as off-the-wall Paki music, but it seemed very natural to us.
Farook Shamsher — The Independent, 1999
Joi Bani comes from the word kurbani: sacrifice. Joi means joyous and victory to the music we make. The vibe we preach is not quite militant, not hippyish. It's just about one vibe with everyone together.
Haroon Shamsher — The Independent, 1999
We can't be disappointed that the media is finally paying attention to the Asian scene because it is what we have worked towards. But I hope it isn't going to be seen as a passing phase.
Haroon Shamsher — The Independent, 1999
We've always attracted a mixed, lively crowd: black, white, Asian, Oriental. Whatever their background, as long as they aren't fighters or fashion victims, they're welcome to join us.
Haroon Shamsher — The Independent, 1999
As far as I know, that was the first regular Asian Underground night. There was always a lot of energy in the place. The brothers would be doing some mad stuff on the decks. They were a pair of characters.
DJ Ritu — Mixmag 2021, on Asian Vibes at Bass Clef
Haroon was a fine and collegiate fellow to be with aboard the tour bus, as well as an attractive and charismatic presence on stage — blessed with an impish sense of humour. His inflight attire included a t-shirt bearing the legend "Don't Panic, I'm Islamic."
Colin Angus — The Shamen
